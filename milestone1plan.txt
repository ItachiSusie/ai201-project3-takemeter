PHASE 2 — EXPLORE RAW DATA AND DISCOVER LABEL CANDIDATES
====================================================================

WHY explore before labeling:
  Do NOT define labels from memory.  Read 30–40 actual comments first
  to see what patterns the real data contains.  Labels that look clean
  in your head often collapse when you read real posts.

STEP 4: Print a random sample of 40 comments
----------------------------------------------
  sample = df["comment_body"].dropna().sample(40, random_state=42)
  for i, text in enumerate(sample, 1):
      print(f"--- {i} ---")
      print(text[:300])
      print()

  While reading, ask yourself:
  - What makes some posts more substantive than others?
  - What would a regular in this community recognize as a "good take"
    vs. noise?
  - Are there distinct modes (e.g., hype, criticism, practical tips,
    genuine analysis)?

STEP 5: Check subreddit breakdown
------------------------------------
  For each of the 4 subreddits, print 10 comments:

  for sub in df["subreddit"].unique():
      print(f"\n=== r/{sub} ===")
      subset = df[df["subreddit"] == sub]["comment_body"].dropna()
      for txt in subset.sample(min(10, len(subset)), random_state=1):
          print(txt[:200])
          print("---")

  Note: discourse style may vary by subreddit.  If one subreddit is
  very different, you may want to sample proportionally from all 4.

STEP 6: Identify label candidates from what you observed
----------------------------------------------------------
  After reading 40+ real comments, write down 3–5 candidate labels
  you noticed.  Common patterns in AI/ChatGPT Reddit discourse:

  CANDIDATE A — hype / uncritical praise
    "ChatGPT will replace all programmers within 2 years."
    "This is literally the most important technology in human history."
    Bold positive claim, no evidence, no nuance.

  CANDIDATE B — criticism / doom
    "This thing is just a fancy autocomplete, it has no understanding."
    "OpenAI is going to destroy society with this."
    Bold negative claim, often exaggerated, no real evidence.

  CANDIDATE C — practical / experience
    "I've been using it to write unit tests — saves me 2 hours a day."
    "Ask it to explain the code before asking it to fix it. Works much better."
    Specific use case, personal observation, actionable.

  CANDIDATE D — analysis / nuanced take
    "The real limitation is context window + hallucination rate on
    factual recall.  Until retrieval-augmented generation matures,
    it's not reliable for research tasks."
    Evidence-based, acknowledges tradeoffs, reasons through a position.

  ► NOTE: After reading your actual sample, you may collapse A+B into
    one "hot_take" label (positive or negative bold claim without
    evidence) and keep C and D separate.  Or you may find the data
    doesn't have enough analysis to justify that label.
    Let the data drive the final label set.

STEP 7: Finalize label definitions
------------------------------------

    2. Two clear example comments from the actual dataset

  TEMPLATE (fill in after Phase 2 exploration):

  LABEL: hot_take
  ---------------
  Definition: A bold, confident opinion about ChatGPT stated without

  The post asserts a strong claim but does not build toward it.

  Example 1: [paste from real data]
  Example 2: [paste from real data]
  Borderline: [paste from real data — what makes you unsure?]

  LABEL: experience
  -----------------
  Definition: A personal account of using ChatGPT for a specific task,

  Example 1: [paste from real data]
  Example 2: [paste from real data]
  Borderline: [paste from real data]

  LABEL: analysis
  ---------------
  Definition: A post that reasons through a position about ChatGPT's
  capabilities or limitations using specific evidence, technical
  context, or comparative reasoning.  The argument is constructed;
  the conclusion is not merely asserted.

  Example 1: [paste from real data]
  Example 2: [paste from real data]
  Borderline: [paste from real data]

  (Optional LABEL: question / discussion — only add if a significant
  fraction of comments are neutral clarifying questions that don't
  fit the above three.)

STEP 8: Check mutual exclusivity
----------------------------------
  Pick 10 random comments and try to assign exactly one label to each.
  For every comment where you hesitate between two labels, write down:
    - Which two labels you considered
    - What rule you used to break the tie
    - Whether that rule also works for the other 9 comments

  If you cannot break ties consistently, one of two things is true:
    A) Two labels overlap — merge them or sharpen the boundary
    B) A new boundary concept is missing — add a decision rule

  RED FLAGS that mean your labels need work:
    - You assign two labels to the same comment more than 20% of the time
    - One label gets 70%+ of your random sample (too dominant)
    - You can't write the boundary rule in one sentence

  MUTUAL EXCLUSIVITY TEST (run this mentally):
    hot_take vs. experience: "Did the person try it themselves, or are
      they making a claim about AI in general?"
      → If personal use → experience.  If general claim → hot_take.

    experience vs. analysis: "Is this just sharing what happened, or
      is the person reasoning toward a conclusion about ChatGPT's
      properties?"
      → If just reporting → experience.  If arguing a point → analysis.

    hot_take vs. analysis: "Would this post still hold up if you
      stripped out the opinion framing and only kept the evidence?"
      → If evidence stands alone → analysis.  If nothing left → hot_take.


====================================================================
PHASE 4 — SAMPLE 200 BALANCED LABELED EXAMPLES
====================================================================

STEP 9: Filter out unusable rows
----------------------------------
  # Drop nulls, very short comments (< 20 chars), and deleted posts
  df_clean = df.dropna(subset=["comment_body"])
  df_clean = df_clean[df_clean["comment_body"].str.len() >= 20]
  df_clean = df_clean[~df_clean["comment_body"].isin(["[deleted]", "[removed]"])]
  print(f"Usable rows: {len(df_clean)}")   # should still be 40K+

STEP 10: Auto-label with keyword heuristics (first pass only)
--------------------------------------------------------------
  Use these heuristics to get a fast first-pass label.
  ALL auto-labels must be manually reviewed in Step 11.

  import re

  def heuristic_label(text):
      text_l = text.lower()

      # Practical / experience signals
      if re.search(r"\b(i (used|tried|asked|told|prompted|got|found)|"
                   r"works (great|well|for me)|doesn't work|my workflow|"
                   r"tip:|trick:|prompt:|use it for|saves me|"
                   r"i've been using)\b", text_l):
          return "experience"

      # Analysis signals — specific technical or comparative reasoning
      if re.search(r"\b(context window|hallucin|retrieval|token|"
                   r"fine.?tun|gpt-[34]|compared to|benchmark|"
                   r"accuracy|limitation|technically|because|"
                   r"evidence|research shows|study|data)\b", text_l):
          return "analysis"

      # Hot take signals — bold claims, extreme praise or doom
      if re.search(r"\b(will replace|already replaced|end of|"
                   r"most (important|dangerous|overhyped|useless)|"
                   r"just (autocomplete|a chatbot|hype)|"
                   r"change everything|destroy|revolutionize|"
                   r"not (real|actual) intelligence|agi|"
                   r"overrated|overhyped|garbage|amazing|incredible|"
                   r"best (thing|tool)|worst)\b", text_l):
          return "hot_take"

      return "REVIEW_ME"   # flag for manual review

  df_clean = df_clean.copy()
  df_clean["auto_label"] = df_clean["comment_body"].apply(heuristic_label)
  print(df_clean["auto_label"].value_counts())

STEP 11: Sample 200 comments — balanced across labels
-------------------------------------------------------
  Target: ~67 per label (if 3 labels).  If 4 labels, ~50 per label.

  TARGET_PER_LABEL = 67   # adjust if you have 4 labels

  sampled_parts = []
  for lbl in ["hot_take", "experience", "analysis"]:
      pool = df_clean[df_clean["auto_label"] == lbl]
      n    = min(TARGET_PER_LABEL, len(pool))
      sampled_parts.append(pool.sample(n, random_state=42))

  # If any label is under-represented, also pull REVIEW_ME rows
  review_pool = df_clean[df_clean["auto_label"] == "REVIEW_ME"]
  sampled_parts.append(review_pool.sample(min(30, len(review_pool)),
                                           random_state=42))

  sampled = pd.concat(sampled_parts).drop_duplicates("comment_body")
  sampled = sampled.sample(frac=1, random_state=42).reset_index(drop=True)
  print(f"Total sampled: {len(sampled)}")

STEP 12: Manually review and correct every label
-------------------------------------------------
  Open the sampled DataFrame.  For each row:
    1. Read comment_body
    2. Check whether auto_label is correct
    3. If wrong, change it to the correct label
    4. If unsure, add a note in the "notes" column

  Fastest review workflow:
    - Export to CSV, open in Excel/Google Sheets
    - Add a "label" column (copy from auto_label to start)
    - Correct labels manually, row by row
    - Add a "notes" column for rows you flagged

  Label distribution target:
    Label         | Target | Acceptable range
    --------------|--------|------------------
    hot_take      |  67    | 55 – 80
    experience    |  67    | 55 – 80
    analysis      |  67    | 55 – 80

  If a label stays under 55 after manual review:
    → Go back to df_clean, pull more REVIEW_ME rows for that label
    → Or pull from a different subreddit in the dataset


====================================================================
PHASE 5 — CREATE THE FINAL LABELED CSV
====================================================================

STEP 13: Build and save the final dataset
------------------------------------------
  After manual review is complete, save the final CSV:

  final = sampled[["comment_body", "label", "notes"]].copy()
  final = final.rename(columns={"comment_body": "text"})
  final = final[final["label"].isin(["hot_take", "experience", "analysis"])]

  # Final sanity check
  print(final["label"].value_counts())
  print(f"Total rows: {len(final)}")
  assert len(final) >= 200, "Need at least 200 labeled examples"

  final.to_csv("data/chatgpt_dataset.csv", index=False)
  print("Saved → data/chatgpt_dataset.csv")

  CSV column format:
    text   — the comment text (comment_body from Kaggle)
    label  — one of: hot_take | experience | analysis
    notes  — optional annotation notes (blank = clean label)

STEP 14: Spot-check mutual exclusivity one final time
------------------------------------------------------
  Take 10 random rows from the final CSV and ask yourself:
    "Could this belong to a different label?"

  For each YES, write down the rule you used to resolve it.
  Add unresolved edge cases to the decision rules in Step 8.
  If more than 3 of 10 are ambiguous → your labels still need work.
